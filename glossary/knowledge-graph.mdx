# Knowledge Graph

**A knowledge graph** is a structured database that stores information as a network of entities and the relationships between them. Google's Knowledge Graph is the most well-known example, but similar structures underpin how large language models organize and retrieve factual information about the world.

When Google displays a knowledge panel for a brand — the box on the right side of search results showing a company's founding date, description, logo, and related entities — that information is drawn from the Knowledge Graph. When an AI platform generates a factually grounded response about a brand, it's drawing on knowledge graph-style associations built during training and retrieval.

## Why It Matters for Brand Visibility

Your brand's representation in knowledge graphs directly affects how AI systems describe you. If the knowledge graph has accurate, complete information about your brand — what category you're in, who founded it, what problems you solve, what markets you serve — AI systems can represent you accurately and confidently.

If your brand entity is sparse or absent, AI systems either skip you or generate responses based on incomplete inference. Neither is good.

## How Brands Get Into Knowledge Graphs

Knowledge graphs pull from structured, authoritative sources:

**Wikipedia and Wikidata** — the most direct path to a strong knowledge graph entry for brands with sufficient notability.

**Schema markup** — Organization, LocalBusiness, and Person schema on your own site feed structured signals directly to search and AI systems.

**Consistent NAP data** — Name, Address, Phone across directories, social profiles, and citations reinforces entity definition.

**Authoritative mentions** — being referenced by sources already in the knowledge graph strengthens your own entry.

## Knowledge Graph vs. Training Data

Language models have their own internal representations of entities built during training — distinct from but influenced by knowledge graphs. Both matter. A brand well-represented in Google's Knowledge Graph tends to be better represented in LLM training data as well, because the same authoritative sources feed both.
